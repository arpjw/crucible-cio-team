# Investment Adviser Registration — Thresholds, Process, and Growth Path

> **Context:** The Investment Advisers Act of 1940 ("Advisers Act") regulates persons who provide investment advice for compensation. If you manage a fund and charge management fees or performance allocations, you are almost certainly an investment adviser. The question is not whether you need to comply with adviser law — it is whether you register with the SEC, register with your home state, or qualify for an exemption from registration. This guide walks through the thresholds, the process, and the path as you scale.

---

## The Three Regulatory Tiers

| Tier | AUM Range | Registrant |
|---|---|---|
| Exempt Reporting Adviser (ERA) | Typically < $150M in private fund AUM | SEC filer, not a registered adviser |
| State-Registered Investment Adviser | Generally < $100M | State securities regulator |
| SEC-Registered Investment Adviser | Generally ≥ $110M | SEC (IARD/EDGAR) |

The gap between $100M (state ceiling) and $110M (SEC floor) is a buffer zone. Advisers in this range have flexibility — they may register with the SEC early or stay state-registered until they hit $110M.

---

## Tier 1 — Exempt Reporting Adviser (ERA)

### Who Qualifies?

An adviser qualifies as an ERA if it:
1. Is an adviser solely to "private funds" (funds relying on Section 3(c)(1) or 3(c)(7) of the Investment Company Act), AND
2. Has less than $150 million in regulatory AUM attributable to private funds in the U.S.

**Definition of private fund:**
- **3(c)(1) fund:** Relying on the 100-investor limit exemption from Investment Company Act registration. Most emerging manager hedge funds and systematic funds start as 3(c)(1) funds.
- **3(c)(7) fund:** Relying on the qualified purchaser exemption. Requires all investors to be "qualified purchasers" (individuals with $5M+ in investments; entities with $25M+ in investments).

### What ERAs Must Do

ERAs must file a **partial Form ADV** with the SEC through the IARD system. The partial filing includes:
- Form ADV Part 1A items relating to the adviser's business and regulatory history
- Certain schedules (Schedule D, Schedule R)

ERAs do **not** file:
- Form ADV Part 2A (brochure) — no delivery obligation to clients
- Full Part 1A items

**Annual updating amendment:** File within 90 days after the adviser's fiscal year end. Most advisers use a December 31 fiscal year, making the annual amendment due March 31.

**IARD filing fee:** $150 for initial registration; $75–$150 for annual updates (varies by AUM tier).

### What ERAs Are Not Required to Do (But Should Consider)

- No mandatory annual compliance review (but best practice to conduct one)
- No mandatory written compliance policies (but advisers Act anti-fraud provisions still apply — Rule 206(4)-7 applies only to registered advisers)
- No custody rule compliance (Rule 206(4)-2 applies only to registered advisers)

**Practical note:** Many ERA-status fund managers voluntarily follow registered adviser compliance practices because state regulators may examine them, LPs expect it, and upgrading to registration later is easier if policies already exist.

## Tier 2 — State-Registered Investment Adviser

### Who Must Register with Their State?

An adviser with less than $100 million in regulatory AUM that does not qualify for an ERA exemption must register with the state in which it maintains its principal office and place of business (and any state where it has more than 5 clients).

**Key exception:** If the adviser has fewer than 15 clients in a state and does not hold itself out as an investment adviser in that state, it may be exempt from that state's registration. Threshold varies by state — check with counsel.

### What State Registration Requires

Requirements vary by state but typically include:

**Firm registration:**
- Form ADV (Parts 1A, 1B, and 2A, through the IARD system)
- Form ADV Part 2A brochure — must be delivered to clients
- State application fee (typically $100–$500 per state)
- Possibly: surety bond (California and some states require bonds for small advisers)
- Possibly: state-specific licensing exams or continuing education

**Individual registration (Investment Adviser Representatives):**
- Each individual who gives advice to clients must register as an IAR in the state
- Form U4 filed through IARD
- Series 65 exam required in most states (see the [Series 65 Study Plan](../formation/series-65-study-plan.md))
- Background check including fingerprinting
- Individual registration fee (typically $30–$200 per state)

**States with additional requirements:**
- **California:** Department of Financial Protection and Innovation (DFPI). Requires a surety bond or net capital for advisers with custody. Active examination program.
- **New York:** Division of Investor Protection. Requires registration if adviser has a place of business in NY, regardless of client count.
- **Texas:** Texas State Securities Board. Examination focus on fee disclosure and advertising.
- **Florida:** Office of Financial Regulation. Annual renewal with CE requirements.

### Form ADV — Part by Part

**Part 1A:** Organizational information, business activities, AUM, disciplinary history, control persons. Filed electronically through IARD. Updated annually and within 90 days of any material change.

**Part 1B:** State-specific supplement. Required for state-registered advisers. Covers additional state regulatory information.

**Part 2A (Brochure):** Narrative disclosure document. Plain English. Must cover:
- Advisory services and investment strategies
- Fees and compensation (exact fee schedules or ranges)
- Performance-based fees (if applicable)
- Methods of analysis and investment strategy
- Risk of loss
- Disciplinary information
- Conflicts of interest
- Brokerage practices (best execution obligation)
- Review of accounts
- Client referrals and other compensation
- Custody (if applicable)

**Brochure delivery:**
- Before or at the time of entering into an advisory contract
- Annually if material changes occurred (summary of changes sufficient if no material changes)
- Upon request at any time

**Part 2B (Brochure Supplement):** Individual-level disclosure for supervised persons who provide investment advice. Covers background, education, disciplinary history, and outside business activities. Must be delivered for each individual who provides advice to clients.

## Tier 3 — SEC-Registered Investment Adviser

### When Must You Register with the SEC?

- Regulatory AUM reaches **$110 million** or more (mandatory)
- Regulatory AUM reaches **$100 million** and you have clients in 15 or more states (may opt into SEC registration)
- You are a "mid-sized adviser" with $100M–$110M AUM that is not required to register and would not be exempt from state registration in your home state — in that case, SEC registration is permitted

**Mid-sized adviser buffer zone ($100M–$110M):** You may register early with the SEC or remain state-registered. Once you reach $110M, SEC registration is mandatory within 90 days.

**Switching from state to SEC:**
- File Form ADV amendment through IARD indicating SEC registration
- SEC becomes effective 45 days after filing (or shorter if SEC approves earlier)
- State registration is withdrawn upon SEC registration becoming effective
- Pay SEC initial registration fee

### What SEC Registration Requires

Everything state registration requires, plus:

**Form ADV Part 1:** Full Part 1A through IARD. More detailed than state version.

**Form ADV Part 2A and 2B:** Same as state, but SEC brochure review standards may differ.

**Annual Compliance Review:** Rule 206(4)-7 requires all SEC-registered advisers to review their compliance policies and procedures at least annually. Must be documented.

**Written Compliance Policies:** Must have written policies and procedures reasonably designed to prevent violations of the Advisers Act.

**Chief Compliance Officer:** Must designate a CCO responsible for administering compliance policies. No minimum experience requirement, but CCO must be competent.

**Custody Rule (Rule 206(4)-2):** If the adviser has custody of client assets (including general partner authority to withdraw assets from client accounts), additional requirements apply:
- Quarterly account statements to clients
- Annual surprise examination by an independent public accountant
- If adviser uses a qualified custodian that sends statements directly, surprise exam may be satisfied by annual audit of the fund with delivery of audited financials within 120 days of fiscal year end
- For pooled investment vehicles (hedge funds/LPs): audit by PCAOB-registered firm + delivery of audited statements within 120 days satisfies the custody rule. This is the standard path for fund managers.

**Performance Fees:** Rule 205-3 permits performance fees (carried interest) only to "qualified clients." Qualified client thresholds:
- $1,100,000 in assets under management with the adviser (adjusted for inflation periodically by SEC)
- $2,200,000 net worth (adjusted for inflation periodically by SEC)
- Knowledgeable employees of the fund

**Advertising Rule (Rule 206(4)-1):** Comprehensive rules on what advisers can and cannot say in marketing materials, websites, pitch materials. Key prohibitions:
- No untrue statements of material fact
- No testimonials or endorsements without specific disclosures (unless exempt)
- Performance advertising must include all related portfoilios if presenting selected accounts
- Hypothetical performance disclosure requirements are extensive and strict
- No cherry-picking favorable time periods without context

## The Path from Exempt to Registered as AUM Grows

| AUM Milestone | Required Action | Timeline |
|---|---|---|
| Fund launched, <$10M | Likely ERA (3(c)(1) fund, private fund AUM < $150M) | File ERA notice with SEC within 60 days of fund launch |
| $25M–$50M | Review state registration requirements; likely state registration required if not ERA | Register in home state within 30 days of triggering threshold |
| $100M | Re-evaluate: state registration ceiling approaching | Begin SEC registration preparation; consult counsel |
| $110M | SEC registration mandatory | Must be registered within 90 days of fiscal year-end assessment |
| $150M private fund AUM | ERA exemption no longer available | SEC or state registration required |

**Annual AUM assessment:** Each year, on the adviser's annual ADV updating amendment date, the adviser must calculate its regulatory AUM and determine whether it should be SEC-registered, state-registered, or ERA.

## Form ADV — How to File (IARD System)

**Step 1:** Create an account on IARD at iard.com (or iard.gov — they redirect to the same system). Requires a CRD (Central Registration Depository) number for the firm and for each individual who will file.

**Step 2:** Obtain a CRD number for the firm by submitting an initial Form ADV electronically through IARD. The system assigns a CRD number upon submission.

**Step 3:** Submit Form ADV Part 2A as a PDF upload through IARD. Part 2A is not structured data — it is a brochure document.

**Step 4:** File Form U4 for each IAR who will be registered. U4 covers individual background and is the basis for state IAR registration.

**Step 5:** Pay state registration fees through IARD's payment system. Fees vary by state and AUM tier.

**Step 6:** State securities regulators review and approve registration. Timeline: 45 days is standard; some states are faster.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Consequence |
|---|---|
| Waiting too long to register (crossing threshold before filing) | Unregistered adviser status — civil and criminal penalties under Advisers Act |
| Failing to update Form ADV within 90 days of material changes | Regulatory deficiency; potential enforcement |
| Using performance data in marketing without required disclosures | Advertising rule violation; SEC enforcement |
| Charging performance fees to non-qualified clients | Advisers Act violation |
| Missing annual ADV amendment deadline | Regulatory deficiency |
| Acting as custodian without complying with custody rule | Enforcement risk; SEC exam red flag |
| No written compliance policies as registered adviser | Immediate deficiency finding in any SEC exam |
