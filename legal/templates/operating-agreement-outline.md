# Operating Agreement — Section-by-Section Outline

> **Purpose of this document:** Use this outline to brief fund counsel before drafting begins and to negotiate key provisions before the first draft arrives. Each section explains what it covers, what decisions you must make before drafting, and what the default vs. founder-friendly vs. investor-friendly positions look like. The goal is to compress billable drafting time by arriving at counsel meetings with positions already decided.
>
> This is an outline for the **management company** Operating Agreement — the LLC that serves as investment manager, general partner, or commodity pool operator. The fund entity (the LP or separate LLC through which investors invest) requires separate offering documents.

---

## Section 1 — Preamble and Definitions

**What it covers:** The formal opening of the agreement, the date of formation, the parties (the members), and a definitions section that establishes the vocabulary used throughout the document. Every defined term appears capitalized in the agreement body; vague definitions cause disputes.

**What you must decide before drafting:**
- Exact legal name of the LLC
- State of formation (Delaware for most funds)
- Effective date
- Full legal names of all initial members

**Key terms to define precisely:**
- "Capital Account" — tracks each member's economic interest; method of calculation matters for distributions
- "Distributable Cash" — cash available for distribution after reserves; how reserves are sized is a negotiating point
- "Manager" — if manager-managed, define who this is and the scope of authority
- "Net Profits" and "Net Losses" — definition affects allocation calculations
- "Transfer" — broad definition prevents members from evading transfer restrictions

**Default position:** Delaware LLC Act defaults apply if no agreement exists. The defaults are mostly member-friendly in ways that do not work for fund management companies (e.g., unanimous consent for transfers).

---

## Section 2 — Formation and Term

**What it covers:** Confirmation of formation under Delaware law, the principal place of business, the registered agent, and the duration of the LLC.

**What you must decide:**
- Will the LLC have a fixed term (e.g., 10 years with optional extensions) or be perpetual?
- Principal place of business address — this affects state registration and tax obligations

**Default:** Perpetual term unless specified otherwise.

**Founder-friendly:** Perpetual term with manager-only right to dissolve. No fixed sunset forces a wind-down discussion.

**Investor-friendly (minority member perspective):** Fixed 5–7 year term with mandatory renewal vote ensures members get a voice in continuation.

**Recommendation for most fund management companies:** Perpetual term with dissolution requiring either unanimous member consent or supermajority (75%+). Avoids accidental dissolution triggers.

---

## Section 3 — Members and Membership Interests

**What it covers:** Who the members are, what percentage each owns, whether membership interests have different classes, and the economic vs. voting rights attached to each interest.

**What you must decide:**
- Membership interest percentages at formation
- Whether to use a single class or multiple classes (Class A: economics only; Class B: voting + economics is a common structure for bringing in key employees or seed investors as members)
- Whether membership interests are expressed as percentages or as units (units are more flexible for future issuances)
- Admission of new members — who can approve, what economics do new members receive

**Default:** Single class, pro rata economics and voting.

**Founder-friendly:** Bifurcated classes where the founder holds a "managing member" interest with full voting control and economic interests are separately allocable. Ensures no investor or minority member can outvote the manager.

**Investor-friendly:** Protective provisions requiring investor consent for material changes (new members above a threshold, changes to fee structure, change of business scope).

---

## Section 4 — Capital Contributions

**What it covers:** What each member contributes to the management company (cash, IP, services), the timing of contributions, whether future contributions are required, and what happens if a member cannot or will not contribute when called.

**Critical point:** Management companies are typically funded by the founders, not investors. The capital structure here is about the manager's working capital, not investor capital (which flows through the fund entity).

**What you must decide:**
- Initial capital contributions by each member (even a nominal amount creates the Capital Account)
- Whether additional contributions can be required (capital calls on members)
- Consequence of failure to make a required contribution (dilution, forced buyout, or default)

**Default:** No required future contributions unless specified.

**Founder-friendly:** No mandatory future contributions; any additional capital is voluntary and dilutive to non-contributing members.

**Investor-friendly (multi-founder):** Mandatory pro rata contributions to fund operations, with dilution mechanism if one founder cannot contribute. Prevents one founder from free-riding on the other's capital.

---

## Section 5 — Allocations and Distributions

**What it covers:** How profits and losses are allocated to member Capital Accounts (allocations) and when and how cash is paid out to members (distributions). These are related but legally distinct: allocations affect tax liability; distributions affect cash flow.

**What you must decide:**
- Profit and loss allocation percentages — do they match membership interest percentages?
- Whether there is a preferred return (hurdle rate) to any member before pro rata sharing
- Special allocations: can the agreement deviate from straight pro rata? (Yes, with §704(b) compliance — requires counsel)
- Distribution timing: discretionary (manager decides) vs. mandatory (on a schedule or upon hitting a threshold)
- Distribution priority: Is any member paid first? Is there a waterfall?
- Withholding for reserves: What percentage of distributable cash is held back for operating expenses?

**Default:** Pro rata allocation and distribution equal to membership percentages.

**Founder-friendly:** Discretionary distributions; founder/manager can retain cash in the entity for reinvestment. No member can force a distribution.

**Investor-friendly:** Quarterly mandatory distribution of all excess distributable cash; limited reserves; priority return to investor-members before performance allocations to founder.

**Tax note:** Allocations must have "substantial economic effect" under Treasury Regulations §1.704-1(b). Your tax counsel must review this section. Misaligned allocations create phantom income problems.

## Section 6 — Management and Voting

**What it covers:** Who runs the company day-to-day (member-managed vs. manager-managed), the scope of the manager's authority, what decisions require member approval, voting thresholds, and meeting and notice requirements.

**This is the most operationally critical section for a fund management company.**

**What you must decide:**
- Manager-managed (a designated manager runs everything) or member-managed (all members vote on all decisions). **Virtually all fund management companies should be manager-managed.**
- Who is the manager? (Typically the founder)
- Can the manager be removed? By whom and on what vote? What triggers removal?
- What decisions are reserved for member vote? (Typical reserved matters: dissolution, sale of the company, admission of new members above a threshold, change of business purpose, manager removal)
- Supermajority thresholds for reserved matters (typically 66.7% or 75%)
- Quorum requirements, notice periods for meetings

**Default Delaware rule:** If no agreement specifies, LLCs are member-managed. This is wrong for most fund managers. Specify manager-managed explicitly.

**Founder-friendly:** Manager has plenary authority except for dissolution and sale. Manager cannot be removed without cause except by unanimous member vote. Cause is narrowly defined.

**Investor-friendly:** Broader reserved matters requiring investor consent; lower removal threshold; cause defined broadly to include underperformance.

---

## Section 7 — Books and Records

**What it covers:** The LLC's obligation to maintain books and records, member inspection rights, accounting methods, fiscal year, and financial statement delivery requirements.

**What you must decide:**
- Accounting method: cash vs. accrual (accrual required for most fund management companies)
- Fiscal year: calendar year is standard
- What records members may inspect (Delaware default is broad — members can inspect records upon reasonable request)
- Whether to limit inspection rights for competitive reasons (feasible but litigated)
- Financial statement delivery: quarterly reports? Annual audited statements?

**Default:** Delaware LLC Act permits member inspection. Management companies often narrow this via agreement.

**Founder-friendly:** Narrow inspection rights to Capital Account balances and tax information only; no inspection of trading records, client lists, or proprietary systems.

**Investor-friendly:** Broad inspection; quarterly unaudited P&L; annual audited financials.

---

## Section 8 — Transfer Restrictions

**What it covers:** When and how a member may transfer membership interests to a third party, right of first refusal provisions, drag-along and tag-along rights, and what events constitute an involuntary transfer (death, divorce, bankruptcy, dissolution of a member entity).

**This section prevents unwanted co-owners from appearing via a back door.**

**What you must decide:**
- Can any member transfer without manager consent? (Almost always: no.)
- Right of first refusal: must transferring member offer the interest to existing members first at the same price?
- Drag-along: if the majority wants to sell the company, can they force minority members to sell on the same terms?
- Tag-along: if the majority sells, do minority members have the right to sell on the same terms?
- Permitted transfers: which transfers are allowed without consent (to family trusts, wholly-owned entities of the member)?
- Involuntary transfer triggers: bankruptcy, divorce, death — what happens to the interest?

**Default:** Delaware allows free transfer of economic rights (but not governance rights) unless restricted.

**Founder-friendly:** Absolute prohibition on transfer without manager consent; founder has drag-along right; no tag-along obligation to founder.

**Investor-friendly:** Reasonable right of first refusal; tag-along rights; death/disability buyout at appraised value.

---

## Section 9 — Dissolution and Winding Up

**What it covers:** The events that trigger dissolution of the LLC, the process for winding up affairs (paying creditors, liquidating assets), and the order in which assets are distributed upon dissolution.

**What you must decide:**
- Dissolution triggers: (1) unanimous member vote, (2) judicial dissolution, (3) term expiration (if applicable)
- Should the manager's death, disability, or removal trigger dissolution? (Typically: no, include "business continuation" provisions)
- Winding up: who manages the wind-down? (Typically: existing manager, or a liquidating trustee)
- Priority of distributions on dissolution:
  1. Creditors (all third-party debts)
  2. Member loans (if any)
  3. Return of capital to members (pro rata)
  4. Remaining assets distributed per profit allocation percentages

**Default:** Delaware requires dissolution upon written consent of all members. Too easy to trigger — restrict this.

**Founder-friendly:** Dissolution requires supermajority member vote; manager's departure alone does not trigger dissolution; founder has the right to appoint a successor manager.

**Investor-friendly:** Dissolution triggered by manager removal for cause, plus mandatory wind-down if no successor manager is appointed within 90 days.

---

## Section 10 — Miscellaneous

**What it covers:** Standard "boilerplate" provisions that are not boilerplate. These provisions govern how disputes are resolved, which state's law applies, and how the agreement is interpreted. Disputes about these clauses happen in real litigation.

**Key provisions:**

**Governing Law:** Delaware law governs the internal affairs of the LLC. If you operate in California or New York, those states may assert jurisdiction over employment and other matters, but the LLC agreement itself is governed by Delaware.

**Dispute Resolution:** Arbitration vs. litigation. Arbitration is private, faster, and not appealable. Litigation is public record but more predictable. For fund management companies, arbitration is generally preferred for disputes with minority members — not because you expect to lose, but because public litigation is reputationally costly.

**Indemnification:** The LLC should indemnify the manager and members for actions taken in good faith on behalf of the company. Define the standard carefully — "good faith" vs. "reasonable belief" vs. "gross negligence" carve-outs.

**Entire Agreement:** Standard merger clause stating this document supersedes all prior oral agreements. Critical if founders made verbal commitments during formation.

**Amendment:** What vote is required to amend the agreement? (Typically: unanimous or supermajority member consent, with administrative amendments by manager alone.) Managers sometimes try to give themselves unilateral amendment power — resist this.

**Counterparts and Electronic Signatures:** Confirms the agreement can be signed electronically and in counterparts. Required for remote closings.

**Severability:** If one provision is unenforceable, the rest of the agreement remains effective.
