# LP Subscription Agreement — Section-by-Section Outline

> **Purpose of this document:** The subscription agreement is the contract between the fund and each investor. Every LP must sign one before their capital is accepted. It does two jobs: (1) it collects legally required representations from the investor to confirm they meet the eligibility thresholds for the offering exemption you are using, and (2) it governs the economic terms of their investment in the fund. Use this outline to brief fund counsel. The subscription agreement is typically paired with a Confidential Private Placement Memorandum (PPM) — the PPM discloses the risks; the subscription agreement collects the investor's acknowledgments.
>
> This outline assumes the fund is structured as a Delaware limited partnership or LLC and is raising under SEC Regulation D, Rule 506(b) (no general solicitation; up to 35 non-accredited but sophisticated investors; unlimited accredited investors).

---

## Section 1 — Investor Representations: Accredited Investor Status

**What it covers:** The investor must affirmatively represent that they meet the definition of "accredited investor" under SEC Rule 501(a) of Regulation D. This is the legal foundation of the offering exemption. If you accept capital from a non-accredited investor and have not met the 35-investor limit and sophistication requirements, you may lose your Regulation D exemption and face SEC enforcement.

**What the fund must verify:** Reasonable belief is the legal standard under Rule 506(b). For Rule 506(c) (which allows general solicitation), verification must be affirmative.

**Current accredited investor categories (as of 2020 amendments):**

*Natural persons:*
- Net worth exceeding $1,000,000 (excluding primary residence), individually or jointly with spouse/spousal equivalent
- Annual income exceeding $200,000 ($300,000 jointly with spouse/spousal equivalent) in each of the two most recent years, with reasonable expectation of the same in the current year
- Holder of Series 7, 65, or 82 license in good standing
- Knowledgeable employee of the fund (for self-fund investment)

*Entities:*
- Bank, registered investment company, registered investment adviser, broker-dealer, insurance company
- Any entity in which all equity owners are accredited investors
- Any entity not formed to specifically purchase the securities with total assets exceeding $5,000,000
- Family offices with AUM ≥ $5,000,000 and their family clients

**What to include in the subscription agreement:**
- Checkboxes for each category with affirmative selection required
- Certification that the selected category accurately describes the investor
- Acknowledgment that the fund may rely on the representation
- Agreement to notify the fund immediately if circumstances change and the investor no longer qualifies

**Why it matters:** Accepting a non-accredited investor under Rule 506(b) requires meeting additional disclosure standards (audited financials, PPM disclosure equivalent to registered offering). Exceeding the 35-investor limit for non-accredited investors voids the exemption. Build a compliance checklist to verify each subscription before accepting funds.

## Section 2 — Investor Representations: Qualified Eligible Person (QEP)

**What it covers:** If the fund trades commodity interests and is using the NFA CPO Exemption 4.13(a)(3), all investors must be "qualified eligible persons" (QEPs) under CFTC Rule 4.7 OR accredited investors. QEP is a higher standard than accredited investor. Many institutional investors qualify as QEPs. If you have investors who are accredited investors but not QEPs, you can still maintain the exemption, but you must track this carefully.

**QEP categories (summarized):**

*Financial instruments test (must own and invest $5M+ in securities and other specified investments):*
- Individuals with $5,000,000 or more in securities and commodities investments
- Entities (not formed for this purpose) with a net worth exceeding $5,000,000

*Portfolio test (alternative):*
- Registered futures professionals and their associated persons
- Commodity pool operators, commodity trading advisors, floor brokers, commodity traders
- Any other person approved by CFTC

**What to include:** Separate QEP checkbox section with the relevant CFTC Rule 4.7 definitions; acknowledgment of the CFTC regulatory context; agreement that if QEP status changes, investor will notify the fund.

**Why it matters:** Under 4.13(a)(3), all investors must be either accredited investors or QEPs. Failure to confirm QEP/AI status before accepting investment puts the CPO exemption at risk.

## Section 3 — Investor Representations: ERISA Status

**What it covers:** Determines whether any investor is subject to ERISA (Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974) or is a "benefit plan investor." If 25% or more of the fund's assets come from "benefit plan investors," the fund's assets become "plan assets" subject to ERISA's fiduciary standards, prohibited transaction rules, and reporting requirements. This is known as the "25% test."

**Benefit plan investors include:**
- Employee benefit plans subject to ERISA (corporate pension plans, 401(k) plans)
- Plans subject to Section 4975 of the Internal Revenue Code (IRAs, Keogh plans)
- Entities whose underlying assets include plan assets (other funds that are themselves above the 25% threshold)

**What to include:**
- Representation identifying whether the investor is a benefit plan investor
- If yes: what percentage of the investor's assets are plan assets
- Acknowledgment that the fund tracks the 25% threshold and may compel withdrawal if threshold is approached
- Representation confirming no prohibited transaction will result from the investment (e.g., fund is not a party-in-interest to the plan)

**Why it matters:** Once the fund tips over the 25% ERISA threshold, the entire fund is subject to ERISA. The manager becomes an ERISA fiduciary, prohibited transaction rules apply to all trading, and additional annual reporting (Form 5500 for large plans) may be required. Many systematic managers intentionally avoid ERISA money to avoid this regulatory overlay.

## Section 4 — Investment Amount and Subscription Process

**What it covers:** The mechanics of how the investor subscribes — the amount, the wire instructions, the closing date, when the investment is effective, and the conditions to acceptance.

**What to include:**
- Subscription amount (must meet minimum investment, typically $250,000–$1,000,000 for emerging managers)
- Wire transfer instructions and confirmation requirements
- Subscription deadline (the date by which subscription documents and funds must be received)
- Acceptance conditions: the fund has the right to reject any subscription in whole or in part, for any reason or no reason, within a specified period
- Effective date: investor becomes an LP as of the applicable closing date (monthly closes are standard; weekly closes are operationally intensive for small funds)
- Pro rata fees: if investor subscribes mid-period, how are management fees and performance allocations prorated?

**What the fund must verify before accepting:**
- All subscription documents signed and complete
- AML/KYC check completed (see Section 5)
- Investor eligibility confirmed (Sections 1–3)
- Funds received and cleared in fund account

## Section 5 — Acknowledgment of Risks

**What it covers:** A series of specific risk acknowledgments. This section does two things: it ensures the investor actually read and understood the risk disclosures in the PPM, and it creates a record that the fund disclosed material risks. Courts have occasionally looked at subscription agreement risk acknowledgments when investors sue.

**Required acknowledgments:**

*Investment risks:*
- Loss of principal is possible; the fund does not guarantee return of capital
- Past performance does not guarantee future results
- Leverage amplifies both gains and losses
- Derivatives involve counterparty risk and basis risk
- Systematic models may fail in novel market regimes

*Liquidity risks:*
- The interests are not registered securities and may not be resold without registration or exemption
- No secondary market exists for the interests
- Redemption is subject to notice periods and lock-up (specify both)
- The fund may suspend redemptions in extreme circumstances (gate provision)

*Regulatory risks:*
- The fund is not registered as an investment company; not subject to Investment Company Act protections
- The manager may operate under exemptions that could change
- Tax laws may change adversely

*Operational risks:*
- Prime broker concentration risk
- Technology and system failure risk
- Key person risk (fund dependent on specific individuals)

## Section 6 — Anti-Money-Laundering Representations

**What it covers:** Required by the Bank Secrecy Act and CFTC/SEC guidance. The fund must have AML policies; the investor representations support those policies and shift liability for false representations to the investor.

**What to include:**

*Investor representations:*
- Investor is not a person or entity named on OFAC Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) list or any similar list maintained by any government authority
- Investor's funds are not derived from illegal activity, tax evasion, fraud, or any activity that would constitute a violation of any law
- The investment does not violate any anti-money-laundering law applicable to the investor
- Investor has not been the subject of any AML investigation or enforcement action
- If investor is an entity: it is not a shell company with no legitimate business purpose

*Fund's rights:*
- Fund may request additional AML documentation at any time
- Fund may compel redemption of any investor who cannot satisfy AML requirements
- Fund may delay any redemption payment pending AML clearance

**What the fund must do (beyond the subscription agreement):**
- Implement a Customer Identification Program (CIP) for all investors
- Check investors against OFAC SDN list before accepting subscription
- Document the AML review for each investor
- Retain AML records for 5 years

## Section 7 — Governing Law

**What it covers:** Which state's law governs the interpretation and enforcement of the subscription agreement, and whether arbitration or litigation governs disputes.

**Standard for Delaware LP/LLC funds:** Delaware law governs. Delaware courts (Court of Chancery in particular) have deep expertise in fund and entity law and are the preferred forum for most fund disputes.

**Dispute resolution options:**

*Arbitration:*
- Private, confidential, faster
- Arbitral award is generally final and not appealable on the merits
- Common: AAA Commercial Arbitration Rules; single arbitrator for disputes under $1M; three arbitrators for larger disputes
- Seat: New York or Delaware
- Advantage: avoids public litigation; sophisticated arbitrators available
- Disadvantage: no meaningful appeal if arbitrator gets the law wrong

*Litigation (court):*
- Public record
- Appealable through normal court hierarchy
- Advantage: more predictable and rule-bound; better for issues of law
- Disadvantage: public; slower; jury trials available in some jurisdictions (waiver of jury trial should be explicit in agreement)

**Recommendation:** Arbitration for investor-fund disputes; litigation for regulatory matters (which are outside the agreement anyway). Include explicit jury trial waiver in either case.

**What to include:**
- Governing law clause specifying Delaware
- Forum selection clause specifying venue (Delaware Court of Chancery or AAA/JAMS arbitration)
- Service of process: investor consents to service by mail at address in subscription agreement
- Waiver of jury trial (explicit)
- Attorneys' fees: prevailing party? Each party bears own? (Prevailing party is more investor-friendly; each bears own is more fund-friendly if you expect frivolous claims)
