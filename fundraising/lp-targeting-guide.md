# LP Targeting Guide — Sub-$10M Systematic Fund

> **Who reads this:** A fund manager who has completed formation, has a live track record, and is ready to begin or formalize LP outreach. The guide covers who invests at this stage, how to find them, what they look for, how to structure the first conversation, and what documents to send at each stage.

---

## Who Invests at Sub-$10M AUM

Most institutional investors have minimum fund AUM requirements that make sub-$10M funds invisible to them. Understanding who your realistic LP universe is saves months of misdirected effort.

### Tier 1 — Friends, Family, and Professional Network (Most Likely First Capital)

**Who they are:** People who know you personally and trust you professionally. Former colleagues, business school classmates, family members with accredited investor status, your former employer's alumni network.

**What drives their decision:** Personal trust in you as an individual, not the strategy. They are betting on you before the track record is long enough to stand on its own.

**What they look for:**
- Confidence that you understand what you are doing
- Confidence that you will not lose all of their money
- Transparent communication — they will worry if they do not hear from you

**Minimum investment:** Often flexible. You set the minimum ($50,000–$250,000 is reasonable at this stage).

**How to approach:** Direct conversation. No cold outreach. Explain what you are doing, what the risks are, and what you are asking. Give them time to decide. Do not pressure.

**What to send:** Pitch deck + subscription documents. No DDQ required — they know you.

**Realistic amount:** $500,000–$2,000,000 from this tier.

---

### Tier 2 — High-Net-Worth Individuals with Alternative Investment Experience

**Who they are:** Accredited investors (often $5M–$50M net worth) who have previously invested in hedge funds, private equity, or venture capital. They understand the risk/return profile of alternatives and they know what a drawdown looks like.

**What they look for:**
- A clear explanation of how the strategy generates returns
- Evidence that the manager understands risk, not just upside
- Differentiation: why this fund vs. a large established CTA
- Alignment: does the manager have personal capital in the fund?

**How to find them:**
- AngelList: not primarily for hedge funds, but the LP base overlaps. Operators who have exited companies often seek alternatives.
- LinkedIn: search for "family office," "private investments," "angel investor" + your city. First-degree connections only — cold outreach requires a pre-existing relationship.
- CAIA network: CFA and CAIA charterholders in non-manager roles (endowments, RIAs, family offices) are often personally accredited and looking at alternatives.
- Your prime broker's cap intro desk: some prime brokers (Interactive Brokers, Goldman, Morgan Stanley) have capital introduction programs. At sub-$10M, access to these programs is limited — ask anyway and understand they prioritize larger funds.

**Minimum investment:** $250,000–$500,000 typical.

**How to approach:** Warm introduction preferred. LinkedIn connection → request 30 minutes to discuss your fund → call → materials if interested. Never send a pitch deck cold.

**What to send (sequentially):** Teaser (1 page) → pitch deck if interest expressed → DDQ if they ask for it → subscription documents.

**Realistic amount:** $500,000–$3,000,000 per individual; 3–6 investors at this tier is achievable with a 6–12 month effort.

---

### Tier 3 — Family Offices with Emerging Manager Programs

**Who they are:** Single-family offices (one family's capital) or multi-family offices (serving multiple families) with explicit mandates to allocate to emerging managers. This is the most sophisticated LP type willing to write checks to sub-$10M funds, and therefore the most competitive.

**What they look for:**
- At least 12 months of live track record (6 months is usually not enough)
- Genuinely differentiated strategy — they see hundreds of trend followers and stat arb funds
- Operational infrastructure that will scale: proper fund admin, auditor, prime broker
- Manager who can answer hard questions without deflecting (they will probe)
- A credible path to $50M+ AUM — they want to grow with you, not be your last investor

**How to find them:**
- **iConnections:** The leading alternative investment networking platform. Create a manager profile. Some family offices use this to discover emerging managers.
- **Context Summits:** Conferences focused on family offices and emerging managers. Attendance is worthwhile at the right stage (roughly $5M+ AUM with a clean track record).
- **AngelList funds section:** Some family offices list their investment criteria publicly here.
- **Seeder database:** Some family offices operate formal seeding programs. Research: Larch Lane (Kellner), Paloma Partners, FrontPoint, Magnitude Capital, Protege Partners. Requirements and terms vary significantly — most require significant prior AUM or pedigree.
- **UHNW advisors:** RIAs and multi-family offices who advise ultra-high-net-worth clients sometimes allocate client capital to emerging managers. Target the alternatives-focused advisors on platforms like iCapital, CAIS, and Titanbay.

**Minimum investment:** $500,000–$2,500,000 typical for emerging manager programs.

**How to approach:** Conference introduction → follow-up email within 24 hours → pitch deck → DDQ → manager reference calls.

**What they will ask that others will not:**
- "Who else is invested? Can we speak with them?"
- "What is your prime broker credit facility? What happens in a margin call?"
- "Walk us through the 2020 vol spike / 2022 rate sell-off / [insert relevant stress event] — what did your model do?"
- "Do you use the same strategy for your personal capital?"

**Realistic amount:** $1,000,000–$5,000,000 per family office; 1–3 family office commitments in year 1 is realistic.

---

### Tier 4 — Fund-of-Funds with Emerging Manager Programs

**Who they are:** Pooled vehicles that allocate to multiple underlying hedge funds. Most large FoFs have $100M+ minimums for underlying funds. The exception is emerging manager programs at specific firms.

**Emerging manager programs worth researching:**
- **Fidelity Emerging Manager Program** — systematic approach; requires track record; competitive process
- **Schwab Emerging Manager** — primarily equity long/short; may not match systematic futures
- **iCapital Network** — technology platform for alternative investment distribution; emerging manager programs through feeder funds
- **CAIA Association** — professional network with emerging manager introductions as member benefit
- **Chicago Mercantile Exchange (CME) Group Emerging Manager Program** — specifically for futures-based strategies; relevant for CTAs
- **Managed Futures Association (MFA)** — trade association; membership provides conference access and some LP introduction activity

**What they look for:** Scalable, institutional-quality operations. AUM of at least $3–5M live. Preference for prior institutional pedigree (Goldman, Citadel, DE Shaw alums get more meetings).

**Realistic amount:** $1M–$5M through these programs in the first 12–18 months.

## The Four-Stage Document Flow

Send documents in stages — sending everything at once signals desperation and creates information overload. Each stage should only proceed when the investor requests the next document.

### Stage 1 — Introduction (no materials)
**Trigger:** Warm introduction or conference meeting.
**What happens:** A 30-minute call or in-person conversation. You explain the strategy in plain English. They ask questions. No documents sent.
**Goal:** Determine mutual interest. Do not ask for a commitment. Do ask: "Would you like to see more?"

---

### Stage 2 — Teaser (1 page)
**Trigger:** LP says yes to seeing more.
**What to send:** A one-page teaser document with:
- Strategy overview (3–5 sentences)
- Since-inception net return, Sharpe ratio, max drawdown
- AUM and target raise
- Team (1–2 sentences)
- Terms (management fee, performance fee, minimum investment, liquidity)
- Contact information
**Purpose:** Filter — the teaser lets the LP decide if the terms and performance profile are interesting before investing time in the full deck.

---

### Stage 3 — Full Pitch Deck + DDQ
**Trigger:** LP reads teaser and requests more detail.
**What to send:** Full pitch deck (see [pitch-deck-outline.md](pitch-deck-outline.md)) + completed DDQ (see [ddq-template.md](../legal/templates/ddq-template.md)).
**Note:** Some LPs will not ask for the DDQ at this stage — wait until they do. Others will require it before any serious meeting. Have it ready.
**What follows:** One or more meetings focused on the pitch deck contents. Expect questions from the DDQ too.

---

### Stage 4 — Subscription Documents
**Trigger:** LP verbally commits and requests documents.
**What to send:** Full offering memorandum (Confidential Private Placement Memorandum) + subscription agreement + any side letters being offered.
**Important:** The PPM and subscription agreement must be prepared by fund counsel. Do not use generic templates for these documents. Errors in the PPM can create securities fraud exposure.

## Structuring the First Conversation

The first conversation is not a pitch — it is a diagnostic. You are trying to determine whether this LP is a realistic match for your fund at your current stage. Most are not. Finding out quickly is a feature, not a failure.

**The agenda for a first call (30–45 minutes):**

| Time | What |
|---|---|
| 0–5 min | Brief introduction — who you are, what the fund does in 2 sentences |
| 5–15 min | Explain the strategy in plain English. Watch for engagement signals. |
| 15–25 min | Ask them questions: What is their typical check size? Have they invested in emerging managers before? What is their decision process? What is their timeline? |
| 25–35 min | Address any initial questions they have |
| 35–45 min | Agree on next steps or confirm it is not a fit |

**Questions to ask the LP (they are not accustomed to being asked):**
- "What is your typical allocation size for a first investment with a new manager?"
- "Have you invested in emerging managers before? What was that experience like?"
- "What would need to be true about a fund for you to make a $[X] commitment?"
- "What is your decision timeline — are you actively allocating now or are you building a pipeline for a future allocation?"
- "What is your redemption expectation — are you looking for liquidity at 30 days or are you comfortable with quarterly?"

**Why ask questions:** Many "interested" LPs are not actually in an allocation cycle. They are educating themselves, filling their network, or running a process that won't close for 18 months. Knowing this early lets you prioritize your time.

## What LPs Look for at This AUM Tier

| What They Prioritize | What This Means in Practice |
|---|---|
| Manager credibility | You, not the fund. Your prior experience, discipline, and clarity of thinking. |
| Transparent communication | Answer hard questions directly. Acknowledge limitations. Do not deflect. |
| Operational seriousness | Real service providers. A real fund admin. An auditor. A prime broker. |
| Risk awareness | More weight on risk management than on return. Show that you know what can go wrong. |
| Realistic expectations | Managers who project 30%+ annual returns are lying or deluded. Both are red flags. |
| Alignment | Your personal capital is in the fund. You eat what you cook. |

| What They Do Not Prioritize at This Stage | Why |
|---|---|
| 5-year audited track record | You are an emerging manager — they know you cannot have this |
| Large team | A solo manager with documented process beats a team with undocumented discretion |
| Institutional prime broker | Interactive Brokers, Wedbush, or similar is fine at this AUM |
| Pedigree | Useful but not required — a clear process and honest communication can substitute |

## Pipeline Management

Track every LP contact in a spreadsheet or CRM from day one. Minimum fields:

| Field | Notes |
|---|---|
| Name | Full name |
| Entity | Firm name / family name |
| Type | HNW / Family Office / FoF / Friend / Other |
| Status | Prospecting / Introduced / Stage 2 / Stage 3 / Stage 4 / Committed / Declined / Paused |
| Last touchpoint | Date of most recent conversation or email |
| Next action | Specific action and date |
| Amount potential | Estimated check size if they commit |
| Materials sent | Which documents and when |
| Notes | Key context — relationship source, objections raised, timeline stated |

**Recommended cadence:**
- Active prospects (Stage 2+): touch monthly minimum. Do not let more than 30 days pass without contact.
- Passive prospects (Stage 1): monthly LP letter is your outreach. Do not cold-call.
- Declined: move to a "closed" list. Note the reason. Revisit in 12 months if circumstances change.

**Key metric to track:** Your conversion rate at each stage. If you are converting 100% of Stage 2 to Stage 3 but 0% of Stage 3 to Stage 4, the pitch deck is fine but the DDQ is failing. Diagnose at stage level.
